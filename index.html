The Problem

Costs increasing at twice the rate of inflation are not the only problems in healthcare’s affordability crisis. Profits are also increasing at twice the rate of costs.

Consolidation and innovation were supposed to deliver “savings” to America’s businesses, but efficiencies have not trickled down to employers.[1]

What prevents employers from benefiting from industry efficiencies is leakage — money the plan spends that it doesn’t know it shouldn't spend. Leakage comes in three forms:

  1. Above-market prices the plan never saw
  2. Low-quality care the plan never revealed
  3. Declining member health risk the plan could not track

The salt in this wound is that the patchwork of siloed vendors addressing one of these three areas self-reports its savings. When they use generalized data to track what they did—and ignore what they could have done—savings are overstated, missed opportunities are not tracked, and vendors are allowed to manage to their own performance guarantees, not to the plan overall.
